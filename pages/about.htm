Our History

The history of immersion learning is technically as old as language itself, but to pinpoint the period of time in which the theory behind it reached its most prominent state, we need look no further than the godfather of the Input Hypthoesis, Stephen Krashen. His theory, introduced in the 1970's, attempted to provide the gordian knot of how we learn languages.

In a nutshell, the Input Hypothesis states that we learn languages through comprehensible input, this is by understanding messages through the language. It is not by hearing words in the language and instantly understanding them, but rather by hearing words in the language and understanding the message they are conveying. This happens through a variety of elements not explicitly tied to the language, audio and visual demonstrations of what message is being conveyed whilst using the language being the most prominent in the earliest stages. A very simple example is if someone repeated the word "gato" whilst holding up a picture of a cat. You may never have heard the word before, but by seeing the image and hearing the word you understood the message.
